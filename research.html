Takeaway 01

Pretrained features are a head start you can't train around

With an identical epoch budget, VGG16's ImageNet-learned edge and texture detectors transferred cleanly to MRI scans: ~95% vs ~80% for the same network trained from random weights.

Takeaway 02

Confidence transferred too, not just accuracy

The transfer-learning model gave sharply confident predictions; the custom CNN stayed uncertain even when it landed on the right class, a meaningful difference for a tool meant to triage scans for radiologists.

Takeaway 03

The pipeline is part of the model

Rewriting the data-loading and preprocessing pipeline cut training iteration time 16.5×, which is what made comparing architectures and augmentation strategies practical at all.

02

The paper

Abstract I · Motivation II · Project description III · Project design IV · Implementation V · Results References

One of the most serious medical issues is brain tumors, which must be diagnosed promptly and accurately to enhance patient outcomes. Automated, AI-driven solutions are necessary because manual brain MRI scan analysis is frequently laborious and prone to human error. This research uses TensorFlow to create a convolutional neural network (CNN) that can identify brain tumors from MRI images. The model trains on labeled datasets to attain high tumor-identification accuracy after preprocessing MRI images to improve quality. Optimizing computational performance, managing imbalanced datasets, and data augmentation were among the main obstacles. By providing a quicker and more accurate tool to help radiologists diagnose brain tumors, the developed system exemplifies the potential of AI in healthcare.

IMotivation

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a key tool in diagnosing and managing brain tumors, but interpreting these scans has its challenges. Even experienced radiologists can occasionally make mistakes due to factors such as the large volume of work they handle daily. A study published in Radiology highlights that subtle abnormalities or high-pressure situations can lead to errors, making the case for tools designed to enhance accuracy and support decision making [1].

MRI's importance in brain-tumor detection is difficult to overstate. It provides detailed images of brain structures, enabling early identification and understanding of tumors, and is particularly good at highlighting soft-tissue differences. Timely MRIs significantly improve patient outcomes by detecting tumors at treatable stages [2]. But the rising demand for scans places increasing pressure on radiologists: managing more volume while maintaining diagnostic standards is increasingly difficult.

Neural networks offer a practical answer. Research shows they can streamline routine tasks (identifying normal scans, flagging abnormalities), letting radiologists focus on complex cases [3]. This project explores convolutional neural networks for exactly that: CNNs detect patterns in MRI scans that are hard for humans to identify consistently [4], and they process large volumes fast enough to matter clinically.

IIProject description

The system classifies brain MRIs into four categories (glioma, meningioma, pituitary tumor, and no tumor) using a labeled dataset split into training and test sets. The dataset was chosen for its diversity and comprehensive representation of tumor types, which lets the model generalize well across the four classes.

The project's core is a convolutional neural network. CNNs automatically extract spatial and hierarchical features from images: convolutional layers pull out local and global features from the scans, while pooling layers reduce dimensionality. TensorFlow implements the architecture end to end (preprocessing, layer definitions, training, and evaluation), chosen for its flexibility, scalability, and built-in tooling for deep learning workflows.

IIIProject design

A · Data preparation

MRI images labeled across the four classes are divided into a training set of 5,712 images and a test set of 1,331 images.

B · Preprocessing

All images are resized to a uniform resolution so every input has identical dimensions, and pixel intensities are normalized to [0, 1], bringing all features to a similar scale so the model learns efficiently and converges faster.

C · Data augmentation

Rotations, flips, zooms, and shifts increase variability within each class. The added diversity reduces overfitting and improves generalization to unseen MRI scans.

D · Model building

Two models classify the same four categories: a custom CNN built from scratch (stacked convolutional layers with ReLU activations, max-pooling for downsampling, batch normalization for stable convergence, dropout for regularization, then dense layers and a four-way softmax) and a transfer-learning model using VGG16 pretrained on ImageNet as a frozen feature extractor with new custom layers on top.

E · Training

Both models trained on the augmented dataset with 20% held out for validation. The custom CNN used the Adam optimizer at a learning rate of 0.001 with categorical cross-entropy loss, early stopping, and a learning-rate-reduction callback. The VGG16 model first trained only its custom head with the base frozen at a lower learning rate of 0.0001, then selectively unfroze base layers for a second fine-tuning pass.

F · Evaluation

Accuracy, loss, and confusion matrices on the held-out test set. The custom CNN classified successfully but needed more epochs and compute; VGG16 showed better accuracy and generalization in less training time, a clear demonstration of transfer learning's advantage for medical imaging.

IVImplementation

The dataset is the Kaggle Brain Tumor MRI Dataset [8]: 7,043 labeled images across the four classes, organized as 5,712 training and 1,331 test images, with the test set further split into validation and test subsets. Images were resized to 224×224 pixels, and the training set was augmented with horizontal flips, brightness adjustments, and small positional shifts.

The custom CNN starts with a convolutional layer of 32 filters (3×3, ReLU) extracting edges and textures, followed by a second convolutional layer of 64 filters (3×3, ReLU) for more complex patterns. Each is followed by 2×2 max pooling and 20% dropout. The feature maps are flattened and passed through a 128-neuron dense layer with batch normalization, then a 64-neuron dense layer with 10% dropout, and finally a four-neuron softmax output.

The transfer-learning model keeps VGG16's convolutional layers (a deep architecture trained on millions of ImageNet images [9]) intact as a feature extractor. Its flattened output feeds an identical head: dense 128 with batch normalization and dropout, dense 64 with dropout, and a four-way softmax classifier.

VResults

After training for 20 epochs, the custom CNN reached an accuracy of around 80%. In the same number of epochs, the model built on VGG16 reached around 95%.

The results demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning for image classification: not only were better results achieved, but higher accuracy arrived in fewer epochs. When classifying images, the transfer-learning model also gave more confident predictions, while the custom CNN carried more uncertainty even when it arrived at the correct classification.
